Overall Conclusion

Anchoring Effect Spectrum: The study reveals a clear pattern in anchoring susceptibility across domains, ordered by effect strength:

1. Highest Vulnerability (r ≈ 0.64-0.59): Abstract/probabilistic tasks (Wheel of Fortune) and monetary valuations (Watch Guess) show strongest anchoring because participants lack strong internal reference points.

2. Moderate Vulnerability (r ≈ 0.39-0.38): Unfamiliar objects and high-familiarity objects both show moderate effects, though for different reasons. Unfamiliarity increases reliance on anchors; familiarity provides some protection but not enough to eliminate bias.

3. Lowest Vulnerability (r ≈ 0.37): Direct observation tasks (age from appearance) show weakest anchoring due to strong perceptual cues and naturally constrained ranges.

Key Finding: All correlations remain statistically significant (p < 0.05), indicating that anchoring effects are universal across domains—they vary only in magnitude. Participants consistently use numerical cues as reference points, even when better information is available. This has profound implications for negotiations, pricing decisions, and judgments in uncertain contexts.
